Who am I?

idk a hoper fr

June 1, 2023 Reid Markland

I hope to appease the kid who always wanted to understand and to help. Growing up I'd always thought the technology we often take for granted seemed like sorcery. The little plastic boxes encasing my TV remote and Nintendo DS seemed to provide access to a mysterious power from a different world. Of course, I was and am a fan of the fantasy genre—especially of the sorcerers trained to manipulate otherworldly power. I hold a special reverence for the archetype of 'the sage' and always strive to be a voice of compromise and wisdom in kind. These ideals married into my drive to uncover the secrets of technology in hopes of affording society the means of achieving sustainability, something desperately needed as we all face climate change. This has only been further cemented through my exposure to the mystical language of mathematics as an essential component of understanding the sorcery of technology.

I hope to understand so that one day I might better help. As the first steps in technological development are often made for applications in physics—many times at the nanometer scale in today's world—I've found my goals align well with a career in the experimental study of solid-state physics. My experiences in the study and research of solid-state physics continually reaffirm this career choice; it all certainly feels like an exploration of otherworldly magic. In graduating from Auburn University this spring with bachelor's degrees in physics and mathematics, I feel the surface of knowledge I'll need has hardly been scratched. Continuing my studies through a Ph.D. in experimental solid-state physics would provide me with the depth of knowledge I feel is needed to contribute significantly to the development of technology today.

I hope to use my understanding to help everyone I can. With the level of corporate presence in the United States, sustainability isn't achievable until it is profitable. Achieving profitability takes place foremost in the research of novel ways to implement commonplace materials in a cost-efficient manner— research like the discovery of graphene in 2004, which won the Nobel Prize in physics in 2010. Alongside the development of more affordable sustainable technologies, communication on how to make such technology profitable is key to achieving a sustainable future. As such, building corporate and political relations is as important as academic relations in this goal. I plan to use the knowledge I gain as a Ph.D. researcher to work at this, spreading sustainability awareness and acting as a bridge between physics' quest for understanding and humanity's technological needs.
